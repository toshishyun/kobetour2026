What is Sake?

Sake is Japan's most celebrated traditional drink — a fermented beverage born from just four simple ingredients, yet requiring extraordinary craft to produce.

🌾
Rice
Special varieties, highly polished
💧
Water
Mineral balance is critical
🍄
Koji Mold
Aspergillus oryzae
🧫
Yeast
Converts sugar to alcohol

Unlike wine, sake cannot be made simply by fermenting fruit. Rice contains starch, not sugar — and yeast can only feed on sugar. This requires an extra step that makes sake brewing more complex and fascinating than almost any other fermented drink in the world.

02

How Sake is Brewed

The full brewing process takes weeks, moving through eight essential stages. During your visit, you may see several of these in action.

1
Rice Polishing The outer layers of each grain are milled away. More polishing = more delicate flavor.
2
Washing & Steaming Rice is soaked, then steamed — not boiled. This matters for texture and starch structure.
3
Koji Making Mold spores are introduced to steamed rice and cultivated in a warm room for 48 hours.
4
Fermentation (Moromi) Koji rice, steamed rice, water, and yeast are combined in a large tank. This is the heart of sake.
5
Pressing The fermented mash is pressed to separate clear sake from solids.
6
Filtering Activated charcoal removes color and off-flavors (some sake skips this for a fuller character).
7
Pasteurization Gentle heating stops microbial activity. Unpasteurized sake — called namazake — is rarer and fresher.
8
Aging & Bottling Most sake rests for 6–12 months before release. Some is aged for years.
03

The Secret Ingredient: Koji

There is a central question in sake brewing:

If rice contains no sugar, how does it become alcohol?

The answer is koji.

What is Koji?

Koji is steamed rice that has been inoculated with Aspergillus oryzae — a mold native to East Asia. As the mold grows, it secretes powerful enzymes.

Saccharification

These enzymes break down rice starch into fermentable sugars. Without koji, there is no sake. Koji is also the foundation of miso, soy sauce, mirin, and many other Japanese foods.

The koji room (muro) is typically the most carefully controlled space in any brewery — warm, humid, and off-limits to visitors at most breweries. The 48 hours of koji cultivation require constant attention and hands-on care by the brewer.

04

Why Sake is Unique

Most alcoholic drinks use a simple process:

Grapes (sugar)
→
Alcohol
Wine — one step

Sake requires two separate conversions — and uniquely, they happen simultaneously in the same tank:

🍄
Koji enzymes Starch → Sugar
(saccharification)
🧫
Yeast Sugar → Alcohol
(fermentation)
✦ Multiple Parallel Fermentation ✦
Unique to sake — this is why it can reach 18–20% alcohol naturally

This elegant biochemical dance, happening simultaneously, makes sake the most alcoholically complex naturally fermented beverage ever devised by humans.

05

Why Nada is Special

Nada has been the center of Japan's sake world for over three centuries. Three factors make it irreplaceable.

1

Miyamizu Water

Miyamizu — "shrine water" — is drawn from natural springs beneath the Nada hills. It is rich in potassium and phosphorus (vital nutrients for yeast) yet remarkably low in iron, which would otherwise dull the flavor. The result: a clean, vigorous fermentation and a sake famous for its crisp, dry character (otokozake — "man's sake").

2

The Rokkō Wind

Cold, dry winds descend from Mt. Rokkō each winter. Sake is brewed in the cold months, and these winds help maintain the low temperatures that allow slow, stable fermentation — the key to complex, refined flavor.

3

Access to Edo (Tokyo)

In the Edo period, Nada's breweries loaded barrels onto ships in Kobe Harbor and sailed east to supply the massive capital city. The long ocean voyage — months at sea — actually improved the sake. Nada grew into Japan's largest sake-producing region as a result.
