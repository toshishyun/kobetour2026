01

Kobe at a Glance

Kobe is squeezed between Mt. Rokkō to the north and Osaka Bay to the south — a narrow ribbon of a city, rarely more than a few kilometers wide. That geography has always pushed Kobe outward: toward the ocean, toward trade, toward the world.

When Japan opened its ports in 1868, Kobe was among the first. Foreigners arrived and stayed. Their traces remain visible today — in the European street grid of the old settlement, a Chinatown, and the unexpected legacy of Russian émigrés who fled their homeland and built something new here.

02

Kobe Port — Where Japan Met the World

In 1858, Japan signed treaties with five Western powers — including Russia — requiring the opening of several ports to foreign trade. Kobe was one of them.

1858
Ansei Treaties signed. Russia among the five powers. Kobe's opening is guaranteed.
1868
Port opens. A planned Western-style Foreign Settlement is established — grid streets, parks, and consulates from across the world.
1881
Russian Consulate opens within the Settlement. For decades, Kobe was where Russians in western Japan came for official matters.

The Settlement's street grid — designed by British engineer J.W. Hart — still exists. Walking through the Kyū-Kyoryūchi district, you are on streets laid out over 150 years ago.

03

Mt. Rokkō — A Mountain Born from Collision

About one million years ago, powerful compression forces pushed the land upward — the Rokkō Upheaval. A range of low hills was forced skyward while Osaka Bay sank. The result: a steep mountain wall rising almost directly from the sea.

Osaka BaySank as the mountain rose

Rokkō's granite is naturally hard — but countless faults have shattered it from within. Weathered over millennia, much of the rock has crumbled into coarse sand called masa. The mountain looks solid. The surface is not.

Rokkō shapes everything in Kobe: its cold winter winds stabilize fermentation in the breweries of Nada; its granite filters rainwater into the city's famously pure drinking water.

04

Nunobiki — The Source of Kobe Water

🚡 If you take the Nunobiki Ropeway Look down as the gondola climbs — the waterfall and reservoir visible below are Nunobiki, the subject of this chapter.

Nunobiki is a narrow valley carved into the lower slopes of Rokkō. Its main waterfall — Otaki, 43 meters — has been celebrated in Japanese literature since the 10th century. The name means "pulling cloth," describing how the water spreads across the rock.

In 1900, Kobe completed a dam high in the valley — Japan's oldest surviving gravity concrete dam, still operating today as a National Important Cultural Property. It was built after a cholera epidemic in 1890 killed over a thousand people and forced the city to build a modern water supply.

Kobe Water · 神戸ウォーター

Ships calling at Kobe took on local water for the long Pacific crossing. Word spread among sailors: this water did not spoil, even crossing the equator.

赤道を越えても腐らない

"Does not spoil even when crossing the equator."

Rokkō's granite filters both Nunobiki's water and Nada's Miyamizu spring water — the key ingredient in local sake. Same mountain. Same geology. Different stories.

05

Russians in Kobe — A Forgotten Chapter

After the 1917 Revolution, Russians who refused Soviet rule — White Russians (白系ロシア人) — fled their homeland. Many came to Japan, and of those, many settled in Kobe. Two of them changed what Japan eats.

Fyodor Morozov
Russia→ Harbin→ Seattle→ Kobe 1924 ★
Opened a chocolate shop on Tor Road in 1926. Introduced high-quality European chocolate to Japan and in 1932 was the first to promote Valentine's Day chocolates here. The brand Morozoff still exists today.
Makar Goncharov
Russia→ Seoul→ Kobe 1923 ★
Set up a chocolate workshop in Kitano in 1923 — said to have been a confectioner to the Romanov imperial court. Created Japan's first whisky bonbons. The brand Goncharoff still operates today.

Both settled in Kitano and Tor Road — a cosmopolitan neighborhood whose streets still carry their legacy. They arrived as refugees and left a mark Kobe still tastes.
